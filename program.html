      Invited Talk: Random walk, synchronization and time-dependent social networks beyond pairwise interactions In the last thirty years network science has proven its strength in modelling many real-world interacting systems as generic agents, the nodes, connected by pairwise edges. Yet, in many relevant cases, interactions are not pairwise but involve larger sets of nodes, at a time. These systems are thus better described in the framework of higher-order networks, whose different orders connections effectively account for multi-body interactions. This intuition has recently opened the perspective to a new branch of network science, involving hypergraphs and simplicial complexes. At this point in history it is worthwhile to re-examine the plethora of systems which have been addressed through network science and wonder how they would appear if assuming a higher-order point of view. Let us consider for instance two of the most simple dynamical processes on networks: random walk and synchronization of oscillatory systems. Both processes are thoroughly described by an apt Laplacian operator in the standard pairwise setting and its generalization to higher-order has been the subject of multiple recent studies. Moving to more applicative scenarios, higher-order networks are useful to study social contexts, in order to seize the intrinsic dynamics of individuals’ behaviors when plunged into a collectivity, where social dynamics are hardly ascribable to pairwise settings and often comprise larger groups simultaneously interacting. Temporal hypergraphs hence qualify as a suitable tool to describe them. By analyzing social interactions datasets we find that higherorder interactions are ubiquitous and, similarly to their pairwise counterparts, characterized by heterogeneous dynamics. In conclusion, it is important to stress how higher-order structures of mutual interactions enrich investigation of both simple dynamical process and human temporal dynamics.

      Invited Talk: The role of higher-order interactions in the dynamics of social contagion and convention Dynamical processes that emulate human behaviors have been the focus of many studies, where social relationships and interactions are typically considered as an underlying structure. As shown recently in various contexts, it is moreover important to take into account the fact that individuals do not interact only in pairs but also in larger groups. Social interactions are indeed a natural testing ground for higher-order approaches. In this talk, I will discuss the effects of considering group interactions on the dynamics of social systems. In particular, I will generalize two models of social contagion and norm evolution, initially introduced and studied on graphs, and now extended as dynamical processes on hypergraphs. Leveraging real-world interaction data and analytical insights, I will show the emergence of novel phenomena such as discontinuous transitions and critical mass effects induced by higher-order interactions. After allowing for the presence of a committed minority, I will show that the ability of committed individuals to overturn an existing norm is non-monotonic in the number of participants in higher-order interactions. These results provide theoretical support to the observation that extremely small minorities can overcome the opinion of the corresponding large majority of the population. Overall, the findings confirm the relevance of going beyond network representations when modeling social phenomena.

      Invited Talk: Flow-based community detection in hypergraphs To connect structure, dynamics and function in systems with multibody interactions, network scientists model random walks on hypergraphs and identify communities that confine the walks for a long time. The two flow-based community-detection methods Markov stability and the map equation identify such communities based on different principles and search algorithms. But how similar are the resulting communities? We explain both methods' machinery applied to hypergraphs and compare them on synthetic and real-world hypergraphs using various hyperedge-size biased random walks and time scales. We find that the map equation is more sensitive to time-scale changes and that Markov stability is more sensitive to hyperedge-size biases.

      Invited Talk: Social contagion models on hypergraphs Our understanding of the dynamics of complex networked systems has increased significantly in the last two decades. However, most of our knowledge is built upon assuming pairwise relations among the system's components. This assumption is often an oversimplification, for instance, in social interactions that frequently occur within groups. For example, in WhatsApp or Telegram groups, the messages follow a one-to-many pattern of transmission. To overcome this limitation, here we study the dynamics of social contagion on hypergraphs, a mathematical object that imposes virtually no constraint regarding sizes. In this talk, we will discuss an analytical framework to study social contagion dynamics on arbitrary hypergraphs. Moreover, we will support our analytical results with numerical results and Monte Carlo simulations. Our analyses show that the model has a very rich parameter space with continuous and discontinuous transitions, bistability, and hysteresis. We will also discuss how this model opens new research paths on higher-order models and the analytical treatment of hypergraphs, posing further questions about modeling dynamical processes on higher-order structures.

      Demo: Temporal networks, modeling infectious diseases, interactive visualizations, and other fancy buzzwords: Three Python packages that might make your life easier The investigation of temporal networks and dynamic systems evolving on them has become a vivid area of research in recent years. Alas, the zoo of open source software that facilitates their modeling and analysis is sparsely populated. Here, I demonstrate the use of the C++(fast)-based Python(simple) package “tacoma” [1], which aims to do exactly that. I’ll show how to convert temporal networks between popular formats, perform simple analyses of their structure and temporal statistics, visualize them interactively, and simulate canonical disease spreading models on them. I’ll continue the demo with “epipack” [2], a Python package that I developed over the last year at Germany’s public health institute to build and analyze complex infectious disease models at lightning speed. “epipack” allows users to easily perform numerical, analytical, and stochastic analyses of complex spreading models, latent or with interactive elements, as well as stochastic simulations on (un/)weighted, (un/)directed networks. Because “epipack” also has a built-in framework to visualize your custom-built spreading models on networks, I’ll showcase “netwulf” [3] in concurrence, which I co-developed with Ulf Aslak to facilitate layouting your static networks interactively in Python.

      Keynote Talk: Higher-order Homophily is Combinatorially Impossible Homophily is the seemingly ubiquitous tendency for people to connect with similar others, which is fundamental to how society organizes. Even though many social interactions occur in groups, homophily has traditionally been measured from collections of pairwise interactions involving just two individuals. In this talk, we develop a hypergraph-based framework to quantify homophily from higher-order, group interactions. This framework reveals that, surprisingly, many homophilous group preferences are impossible; for instance, men and women cannot simultaneously exhibit preferences for groups where their gender is the majority. This is not a human behavior but rather a combinatorial impossibility of hypergraphs. We will show that relaxed notions of group homophily appear in numerous contexts, albeit with subtleties. For example, U.S. members of Congress exhibit strong tendencies for cosponsoring bills with same-party cosponsors, but in order for this to be possible, there must be a substantial number of individuals from each party that are willing to cosponsor bills when their party is in the minority. We also examine gender distribution in group pictures with varying group sizes and find several differences from graph-based measures.

      Invited Talk: A bounded-confidence model on hypergraphs In the study of opinion dynamics on networks, one often encodes interactions between people in the form of dyadic relationships, but many social interactions in real life are polyadic (i.e., they involve three or more people). In this talk, I'll discuss our extension of an asynchronous bounded-confidence model (BCM) to hypergraphs. We show that our hypergraph BCM converges to consensus under a wide range of initial conditions for the opinions of the nodes. We also show that there is a phase transition in the convergence time on the complete hypergraph when the variance of the initial opinion distribution equals the confidence bound c. Therefore, to determine the convergence properties of our hypergraph BCM when the variance and the number of hyperedges are both large, it is necessary to use analytical methods instead of relying only on Monte Carlo simulations. Finally, we also observe a phenomenon that is not possible in standard dyadic BCMs: the opinions of individuals can sometimes jump from one opinion cluster to another in a single time step (we call this “opinion jumping”). This work is a collaboration with Abigail Hickok, Michelle Feng, Yacoub Kureh, and Mason Porter.

      Invited Talk: Detectability of Communities in Heterogeneous Hypergraphs The celebrated detectability threshold describes regimes of graphs in which no algorithm can detect the presence of planted communities, even when those communities are truly present and statistically distinct. Several recent papers have offered detectability results for communities in hypergraphs. Those results, however, focus on the case of uniform hypergraphs — hypergraphs in which all edges have the same cardinality. Since most hypergraph data is non-uniform, overcoming the uniformity assumption is an important next step. In this talk, I’ll first give a brief overview of detectability in graphs and uniform hypergraphs. I’ll then describe some ongoing work (joint with Jamie Haddock and Nicole Eikmeier) to extend the theory of hypergraph detectability via higher-order nonbacktracking operators. We offer extensions of the Ihara-Bass determinant formula to two classes of nonbacktracking operators, as well as experimentally-supported results regarding the location of the detectability threshold in non-uniform hypergraphs.
      16:00 - 16:30 Lee DeVille (Department of Mathematics, University of Illinois at Urbana-Champaign, USA)
      Invited Talk: Consensus on simplicial complexes --- results on stability and synchronization We consider a nonlinear flow on simplicial complexes generated from the simplicial Laplacian, and show that it is a generalization of various consensus and synchronization models commonly studied on networks. In particular, our model allows us to formulate flows on simplices of any dimension, so that it includes edge flows, triangle flows, etc. We show that the system can be represented as the gradient flow of an energy functional, and use this to deduce the stability of various steady states of the model. Finally, we demonstrate that our model contains higher-dimensional analogues of structures seen in related network models.
